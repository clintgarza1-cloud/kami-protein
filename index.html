90 grams, three feedings, veggie-forward.

A protein plan tuned for Kami — built to hold lean mass and protect her hair on Wegovy. Veggie-forward by preference, but a little animal protein is welcome and often the smartest lever to clear each feeding.

90g protein / day ≈30g per feeding ≥2.5g leucine each veggie-forward

Why these numbers

The medication changes the math.

General meal-prep guidance, not medical advice — her prescriber or a registered dietitian sets anything personalized, especially protein targets, iron, and supplements.

Wegovy quiets appetite, so she'll eat less by volume. The risk isn't fat — it's that a real slice of any weight lost comes off as muscle and hair unless protein stays high and well-timed. Every bite has to earn its place.

Hold the muscle

Protein is the anchor

On a GLP-1, roughly 25–40% of weight lost can be lean tissue (about 40% in the semaglutide STEP-1 trial). Treat ~90g/day as a floor, not the finish line, spread across the day — not stacked into one meal — to tell the body to keep muscle instead of burning it. Muscle-preservation guidance runs ~1.2–1.6g protein per kg of body weight (higher if she's lifting); clinicians base that on ideal or adjusted body weight, not the scale number, so have her prescriber or a dietitian set the exact target — and pair it with 2–3×/week resistance training, the single biggest lever for keeping muscle.

Keep the hair

Shedding is preventable

Hair loss on Wegovy is mostly rapid-loss + under-nutrition, not the drug itself. Steady protein plus iron, zinc, and enough calories is the defense. It usually settles once intake is solid.

The switch

Leucine flips the signal

One amino acid — leucine — is the trigger for building muscle. Each feeding needs ~2.5–3g of it to "turn on." Below that, the protein mostly just passes through.

The plant catch

Combine to complete

Most plants are lighter in leucine and miss an amino or two. Pairing sources (legume + grain + soy, or a pea-rice blend) closes the gaps. And the cheapest way over the line is a small touch of real animal protein — one egg, a splash of milk, grated parmesan, or a half-scoop of whey.

The leucine line

Why a plate of beans isn't enough on its own.

Animal and whey protein clear the leucine threshold easily — a single scoop of whey does it. Plant feedings get there too, they just need either more total grams or a smarter combination. The builder below makes that visible: each feeding has to cross the white line.

2.5g — switch flips

Left of the line, muscle-building stays mostly idle. Cross it and the feeding "counts." Whey gets there on its own; a plant plate usually needs a legume + a soy food + a grain. The simplest lever per gram is a small bump of real animal protein — one egg, a splash of milk, or a half-scoop of whey — or a small EAA top-up.

One caveat: not all animal "protein" counts. Collagen — bone broth, gelatin — is low in leucine and missing tryptophan, so it barely moves the muscle signal no matter how many grams it shows. Good for flavor and skin, not for the switch.

The three feedings

Prep blueprints.

1 · The shake ≈31g protein · 2.7g leucine

  • Pea + rice protein blend (or whey isolate)1 scoop
  • Soy milk — adds complete protein & leucine1 cup
  • Frozen banana or berries½ cup
  • Honey1 tsp
  • Ice + splash of waterto blend

Why it works: soy milk instead of water is the quiet upgrade — it pushes the blend over the leucine line and makes it creamier. The honey/fruit is the bribe.

Swap → oat milk drops the protein; keep it soy or dairy.

2 · The plate ≈33g protein · 3.1g leucine

  • Tempeh, cubed & pan-crisped (leucine MVP)½ cup
  • Lentils, simmered¾ cup
  • Quinoa, cooked½ cup
  • One egg, stirred through warm (or grated parmesan)1 large
  • Edamame or roasted veg, folded inhandful
  • Nutritional yeast + olive oil to finishto taste

Why it works: tempeh + lentil + quinoa already build a near-complete amino profile, and the small real-protein add — an egg stirred through off the heat, or a handful of grated parmesan — is the efficient lever that pushes leucine cleanly over the line. A splash of milk or a half-scoop of whey does the same job.

Want broth? → simmering the grains in bone broth is fine for flavor and a little collagen for skin — but collagen is low-leucine and incomplete, so it does not count toward the muscle signal. Use the egg/dairy for that.

No tempeh? → firm tofu scramble + black beans + an egg or parmesan hits similar. Fully plant version → drop the egg, lean on tempeh + edamame + a half-scoop of soy isolate to clear the line.

3 · The snack stack ≈30g protein · 2.6g leucine

  • Greek yogurt or whipped cottage cheese (if dairy's ok)1 cup
  • Lemon protein gummies (recipe below)1 serving
  • Hemp seeds + berries on top2 tbsp

How it clears: real dairy — Greek yogurt or whipped cottage cheese — carries this feeding over the line on its own. Plant option: swap in a soy yogurt + a half-scoop of protein stirred in, then the gummies and hemp finish it. Either way, the gummies alone won't carry a feeding — they're a topper, not the base.

Dairy-free → soy yogurt is the only plant yogurt with real protein; almond/coconut won't count.

Home recipe

Lemon protein gummy bears.

The 1Up-style trick — but built to actually carry protein. Gelatin sets them but is incomplete on its own, so the whey (or soy) isolate is what makes them count. Makes roughly one snack-serving's worth of protein per batch.

Lemon gummies ≈12–15g protein / batch

  • Cold water or lemon juice½ cup
  • Unflavored gelatin (sets it)3 tbsp
  • Lemon protein isolate (whey or soy)1 scoop
  • Fresh lemon juice2 tbsp
  • Honey or a pinch of sweetenerto taste

Method: sprinkle gelatin over the cold liquid, let it bloom 2 min. Warm gently on low until clear — don't boil (kills the set and clumps the protein). Off heat, whisk in the protein, lemon juice, and sweetener until smooth. Pour into bear molds, chill 1–2 hrs.

For a vegan set → swap gelatin for agar (use ~1 tsp agar per cup liquid; it sets firmer and does need a brief boil before adding protein once slightly cooled).

Hair insurance

Beyond protein, the four that matter.

Protein is the foundation, but veggie-forward + rapid loss is exactly the combo that thins hair. These cover the usual gaps. Worth a quick check with her prescriber, especially iron, before supplementing.

  • Iron — the big onePlant (non-heme) iron absorbs poorly; rapid weight loss drains stores. Pair iron-rich foods (lentils, tofu, pumpkin seeds, spinach) with vitamin C, and ask about checking ferritin — low iron is the most common hidden cause of shedding.
  • ZincPumpkin seeds, hemp, legumes, cashews. Directly tied to the hair-growth cycle and easy to run low on without meat.
  • Enough calories, not too fastHitting protein but eating far too little overall still triggers shedding. The goal is steady loss, not a crash — protein-dense feedings help her get there without volume.
  • Biotin + B-complexRarely truly deficient, but a B-complex is cheap insurance on a veggie-forward diet (B12 especially matters). Don't megadose biotin — it can skew lab results.

This is a meal-prep planning guide, not medical advice. Targets here are general — her prescriber or a dietitian should set anything personalized, especially around protein targets, iron, supplements, and how fast she's losing.
